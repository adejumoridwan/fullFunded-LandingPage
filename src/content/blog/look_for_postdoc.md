If you have spent any time searching for postdoc funding, you already know the usual suspects. The NIH Ruth L. Kirschstein fellowship. The NSF Postdoctoral Research Fellowship. The Marie Skłodowska Curie Actions. These are the big names that career advisors point to, the ones that fill up every "postdoc funding guide" you find online.

But here is something that most of those guides will not tell you: a significant portion of postdoc positions are funded through money that never appears on any portal. It sits in departmental accounts, lives inside individual lab budgets, and flows through grant mechanisms that are almost invisible unless you know exactly where to look.

This guide is about that invisible funding. It is for the researcher who has already bookmarked ResearchGate, checked Euraxess, and worked through the standard fellowship databases, and is now wondering why the landscape still feels so thin.

## Why So Much Postdoc Funding Goes Unannounced

Before we get into the specific strategies, it is worth understanding why this funding is hidden in the first place. It is not because principal investigators are trying to keep it secret. It is simply how academic funding works structurally.

When a PI receives a large R01 grant, an ERC consolidator award, or a Wellcome Trust investigator grant, that funding often includes personnel lines that are not yet filled. The PI may know they want to hire a postdoc, but they have not formally posted the position yet. They might be waiting to see how a current project develops, or they may simply prefer to hire someone through word of mouth and direct contact rather than sifting through hundreds of applications.

Similarly, many universities receive block grants, institutional training grants, and philanthropic endowments that generate postdoc stipend funding. This money is sometimes allocated centrally, but often it is pushed down to individual departments or even individual faculty members who then decide how to deploy it.

The result is a funding ecosystem where a meaningful share of available positions are never formally advertised, and the researchers who access them are typically those who were already embedded in the right networks or knew to ask the right questions.

The good news is that you do not have to be an insider by birth. You can become one strategically.


## Start With Grant Databases, Not Job Boards

Most researchers approach postdoc funding by looking at job boards. This is understandable but backwards. Job boards show you positions that already exist and have already been through an internal approval process. By the time a postdoc position appears on a jobs board, the pool of well-networked candidates has often already made contact with the PI.

A more effective starting point is grant databases, which show you who has money right now and what they are funded to work on.

**NIH RePORTER** is the most powerful free tool available for researchers in the life sciences. It is a publicly searchable database of all active NIH-funded projects. You can filter by institution, by year, by mechanism, by disease area, or by specific keywords related to your research. When you find a PI running a multi-year R01 or R01-equivalent grant that was awarded in the last two to three years, that PI almost certainly has personnel budget available. They may not have posted anything publicly, but they are funded to hire.

The same logic applies outside the US. **UKRI's Gateway to Research** provides a similar searchable database for UK-funded projects. The **European Commission's CORDIS** database covers Horizon Europe and predecessor programmes. The **Australian Research Council's research grants database** covers ARC projects.

These are not funding opportunities in the traditional sense. They are intelligence resources. You are not applying to anything. You are building a map of who has active funding in your area so that you can reach out to them directly, which we will get to shortly.


## Understand the Grant Mechanisms That Almost Always Include Postdoc Lines

Not all grants are created equal when it comes to postdoc funding. Understanding which mechanisms typically include postdoc salaries will help you focus your searching.

- **R01 grants (NIH)** are the workhorses of American biomedical research, and a standard R01 budget almost always includes at least one postdoc position. If you find a PI who received a new or renewed R01 in the past 18 months, there is a good chance that postdoc line is either unfilled or will be vacated soon.

- **NIH P01 and P50 Program Project and Center grants** are even more postdoc-rich. These are collaborative, multi-investigator grants, and they routinely include multiple postdoc positions distributed across participating labs. Because these are larger and more complex to administer, the hiring process is often more informal and relationship-driven.

- **NSF CAREER awards** are five-year grants given to early-career faculty, and they typically include funding for graduate students and sometimes postdocs. A faculty member who received a CAREER award in year two or three may be at exactly the stage where they are ready to bring on a postdoc to help move the project forward.

- **T32 Training Grants (NIH)** are institutional training grants that fund predoctoral and postdoctoral trainees. These are administered at the departmental level, and the slots are often allocated by a steering committee rather than by individual PIs. The important thing to know is that these grants exist and that you can ask a department coordinator whether they have T32-supported postdoc positions available. This is not information that is always surfaced automatically.

- **ERC Starting and Consolidator Grants** in Europe are similar. They are large, prestigious, and they almost always include postdoc positions. Searching CORDIS for recently awarded ERC grants in your field will give you a list of PIs who have confirmed funding.

## The Quiet Power of Departmental and Divisional Funds

Beyond individual PI grants, many institutions maintain departmental funding pools that are used to recruit exceptional postdoctoral researchers. These pools are often funded through overhead recovery from grants, through endowments, or through discretionary funds held by department chairs.

These positions are rarely advertised. They are typically offered to candidates who come to the department's attention through other means, whether through a seminar presentation, a conference introduction, or a direct inquiry that comes at the right moment.

The way to access these funds is simple in principle, even if it requires some confidence to execute in practice. Contact the department administrator or the director of graduate studies and postdoctoral affairs at departments where you would want to work. Tell them you are a researcher finishing your PhD or a postdoc looking for your next position, describe your research briefly, and ask directly whether the department has any internally funded postdoc opportunities or mechanisms they support.

Some departments will say no. Others will say they do not have anything right now but will keep your information on file. A few will tell you something useful, maybe that there is a named fellowship you had not found, or that a particular PI has just received a grant and is about to start hiring.

The ratio of effort to return is favorable here because almost nobody does this. Most researchers limit their search to formal postings. Simply asking the question puts you in a category of motivated, proactive candidates that departments remember.


## Foundation and Society Grants That Fund Lab-Embedded Postdocs

A category of funding that is genuinely underused by postdocs is the landscape of foundation and learned society grants that are designed to be held within an existing lab rather than to support an independent fellowship position.

These mechanisms work differently from named fellowships. Instead of the postdoc applying for independent funding and then negotiating with a host lab, the PI applies on behalf of the postdoc, or the postdoc and PI apply jointly, with the understanding that the position exists within a specific lab context.

Some examples worth knowing about:

- **Damon Runyon Cancer Research Foundation** awards are highly competitive but cover salary and expenses for postdocs working on cancer-relevant research. The award is held by the postdoc but is tied to a specific sponsoring lab.

- **American Heart Association Postdoctoral Fellowships** follow a similar model for cardiovascular research. The postdoc identifies a mentor and a project, and the application is submitted through the host institution.

- **Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI) Hanna H. Gray Fellows Program** is aimed specifically at increasing diversity in the life sciences research workforce. HHMI Investigator labs are natural places to look for these opportunities.

- **Wellcome Trust Early Career Awards** in the UK can support researchers who are transitioning from postdoc to independent positions, and some Wellcome-funded labs also have discretionary funding for postdocs embedded in their programs.

- **Disease-specific foundations** are perhaps the most overlooked source of postdoc funding. Organizations focused on conditions like ALS, Parkinson's disease, cystic fibrosis, rare pediatric cancers, and many others often have postdoctoral award mechanisms that receive far fewer applications than equivalent programs at major funding bodies. The Alzheimer's Association, the Muscular Dystrophy Association, and the Multiple Sclerosis Society all have postdoc funding programs. If your research has any connection to a specific disease community, it is worth searching that community's foundation for fellowship opportunities.

## Industry-Funded Academic Postdocs

This is a category that academic researchers sometimes overlook because it feels like it sits at the boundary of academia and industry, which can trigger anxiety about career trajectory. But industry-funded academic postdoc positions can offer genuine intellectual freedom alongside better compensation and an expanded professional network.

Many pharmaceutical, biotech, and technology companies fund academic postdoc positions either through direct research collaborations with specific PIs or through formal programs. These positions are typically administered through the university, which means the postdoc maintains access to academic resources and can publish their work, subject to agreed review timelines.

The way to find these is to look at the acknowledgments sections of papers published by labs you are interested in. When a paper thanks a specific company for funding support, that is a signal that a research relationship exists. The PI may have access to additional positions through that relationship, or they may be able to facilitate an introduction.

Separately, some companies maintain formal academic liaison programs. Pfizer, Novartis, and several other large pharmaceutical companies have historically run postdoc fellowship programs that are jointly administered with academic partners. These programs are not always heavily advertised and are worth searching for directly on company research websites.


## How to Reach Out to PIs About Unfunded Positions

Finding out that a PI has active grant funding is only half of the equation. You still need to reach out and make the case for yourself without sounding like you are cold-calling for a job that does not exist.

The framing matters enormously here. An email that says "I am looking for a postdoc position and saw that you have funding" is likely to go straight to the trash. An email that demonstrates genuine engagement with the PI's work and proposes a specific scientific question or collaboration is something most researchers will take seriously.

A few principles for this kind of outreach:

- **Be specific about the science.** Read at least three recent papers from the lab and identify a specific question, result, or direction that connects to your own work. Your email should make it obvious that you have done this reading and that you have something genuine to contribute.

- **Lead with what you bring, not what you want.** Instead of opening with your job search situation, open with a brief description of your research and why it is relevant to the PI's program. The implicit message is that collaborating with you would advance their scientific goals, not just solve your employment problem.

- **Ask for a conversation, not a position.** "I would welcome the opportunity to speak with you about whether there might be a fit between my research interests and your lab's work" is a much lower-stakes ask than "I am applying for a postdoc in your lab." It reduces the activation energy for a busy PI to respond.

- **Be honest about your timeline.** If you are finishing your PhD in six months, say so. If you are currently a postdoc looking for your next move, explain that briefly. This context helps the PI assess whether the timing could work.


## Using Conference Networks to Unlock Informal Opportunities

The most reliable path to lab-specific postdoc funding that is not formally advertised runs through personal relationships, and conferences remain the most efficient place to build those relationships quickly.

The key is to approach conference networking with a specific agenda. Before you attend, identify five to ten PIs whose work you find genuinely exciting and who have active funding in your area. Read their recent papers carefully. At the conference, look for opportunities to engage with them around specific scientific content rather than asking about jobs directly.

A conversation that starts with "I found your recent paper on X fascinating, particularly the finding that Y, which connects to something I observed in my own work on Z" is both more authentic and more likely to lead somewhere useful than one that opens with "I am looking for a postdoc."

Poster sessions and social events at conferences are particularly good for this. Most PIs are genuinely happy to talk about their science, and a substantive conversation at a conference will make any subsequent email follow-up far more likely to receive a response.

## The International Angle: Bilateral Funding Mechanisms

If you are open to international positions, a set of funding mechanisms specifically designed for international mobility are often dramatically undersubscribed. These bilateral agreements between research councils in different countries create postdoc opportunities that can be difficult to find through standard searches.

**Fulbright Scholar Program** for outbound US researchers and the various receiving-country equivalents for inbound researchers are the most prominent example, but there are many others. The **DAAD** funds international researchers coming to Germany and German researchers going abroad. The **Japan Society for the Promotion of Science (JSPS) Postdoctoral Fellowship** accepts applications from non-Japanese researchers to conduct research in Japanese labs and is genuinely competitive but receives far fewer applications than its American and European equivalents.

**Newton Fund partnerships** between the UK and partner countries created a range of bilateral mobility fellowships that are worth searching for if you have research connections in any of the partner countries.

The practical approach is to identify countries where research in your field is strong, then search directly for the national research council or science ministry of that country plus the terms "international postdoctoral fellowship" or "visiting researcher." Many of these programs have English-language application portals and actively seek international candidates.


## Keeping Track of What You Find

As you work through these strategies, you will accumulate a large and somewhat chaotic collection of leads, names, grants, programs, and potential contacts. A simple tracking system will keep you from losing useful information.

A spreadsheet with columns for the PI name, institution, grant mechanism, grant period, relevant research area, contact status, and notes will serve you better than a collection of bookmarks and browser tabs. When you find a PI who has active funding and whose work connects to yours, log them immediately with whatever information you have. This gives you a working list of people to reach out to over time rather than trying to do everything at once.

Aim to reach out to three to five PIs per week rather than blasting a hundred emails at once. A smaller number of genuinely personalized, well-researched outreach messages will produce far better results than a mass-contact approach.

## A Note on Persistence and Timing

One of the most important things to understand about this kind of search is that timing is almost entirely outside your control, and rejection or non-response in this context rarely means anything about your quality as a researcher. A PI who has no budget this month might have a postdoc leave unexpectedly in three months. A lab that was fully staffed when you reached out might have a position open up around the time you finish your current position.

For this reason, it is worth maintaining relationships and keeping lines of communication open even when an initial inquiry does not lead anywhere. A brief follow-up email six months later, especially if you have a new paper out or have presented at a conference where their work came up, is entirely appropriate and sometimes catches someone at exactly the right moment.

The researchers who successfully navigate the hidden postdoc funding landscape are not necessarily the most brilliant or the most qualified. They are often simply the ones who cast a wider net, stayed organized, and kept going long enough for timing to work in their favor.


## Summary: Where to Start

If you are putting this into practice today, here is a sensible sequence to follow.

Begin with grant database searches. Spend two hours in NIH RePORTER, Gateway to Research, or CORDIS depending on your field, and build a list of 20 to 30 PIs who have received relevant funding in the past two years. Note the grant mechanism and the funding period so you can assess how much runway the lab likely has.

Then look for disease-specific foundations and learned societies relevant to your research area and inventory their postdoctoral award mechanisms. Many of these will be things you have never heard of, and some will have upcoming deadlines worth targeting.

Next, identify two or three departments at institutions where you would genuinely want to work and send polite, brief inquiries to the department administrator asking about internally funded opportunities.

Finally, identify an upcoming conference where several of the PIs on your list will likely be presenting, and use that as a target for relationship-building conversations.

None of this replaces the standard fellowship applications. It runs alongside them. The researchers who land the best postdoc positions are usually doing both simultaneously, pursuing named fellowships through official channels while also cultivating the informal relationships and direct outreach that surface opportunities the portals will never show.

The funding exists. It is just not always waiting where everyone else is already looking.
